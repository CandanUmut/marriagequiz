# Evidence-based foundations for a marriage compatibility platform

**The strongest predictors of marital success are not who partners are individually but how they function together** — a finding that fundamentally challenges how compatibility platforms are typically designed. Across 43 longitudinal datasets encompassing 11,196 couples, Joel et al. (2020, *PNAS*) found that relationship-specific variables like perceived partner commitment, appreciation, and sexual satisfaction explain **45% of variance** in relationship quality, while one partner's individual traits explain only **5%** of the other's satisfaction. This means the most powerful predictors cannot be assessed before two people meet. Yet pre-meeting assessment is not futile: certain dimensions — financial alignment, core values, family planning, and communication capacity — consistently predict who stays married and who does not, often with large effect sizes. The platform design challenge is distinguishing what can be meaningfully assessed in advance from what cannot.

## Area 1: What longitudinal research reveals about lasting marriages

### The predictors that hold up across decades of study

Four independent research programs converge on the strongest predictors of marital longevity and satisfaction:

**Communication and conflict patterns** remain the most robust predictor category. Gottman and Levenson's observational studies found that four destructive communication patterns — criticism, contempt, defensiveness, and stonewalling — predicted divorce with claimed **93.6% accuracy** (Gottman, 1994, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*). Of these, **contempt** is the single greatest predictor of divorce, particularly within the first six years. The demand-withdraw pattern (one partner pressing for discussion while the other withdraws) shows a meta-analytic effect of **r = .36** for predicting negative relationship outcomes across 74 studies and 14,255 participants (Schrodt et al., 2014). However, the Karney and Bradbury 2020 decade review (*Journal of Marriage and Family*) introduced an important caveat: negative communication "can be difficult to change, does not necessarily lead to more satisfying relationships when it is changed, and does not always predict distress in the first place." **Evidence strength: Strong, with important caveats.**

**Neuroticism** emerged from the landmark Karney and Bradbury (1995) review of 115 longitudinal studies representing over 45,000 marriages as the personality trait most consistently associated with lower marital satisfaction, explaining approximately **10% of variance**. A 13-year longitudinal study confirmed that high neuroticism predicted negative marital interaction, which reduced both partners' satisfaction. Conscientiousness becomes increasingly important over time in long-term marriages (Claxton et al., 2012, *Journal of Social and Personal Relationships*). **Evidence strength: Strong.**

**Disillusionment, not distress, distinguishes divorcing couples.** Ted Huston's landmark PAIR Project followed 168 couples for 13 years from courtship through marriage (Huston et al., 2001, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*). The decline of love, affection, and the rise of ambivalence — not increasing negativity — distinguished couples who divorced from those who stayed married. This "disillusionment model" suggests that erosion of positive connection matters more than escalation of conflict. **Evidence strength: Strong.**

**Most couples' satisfaction does not decline over time.** Challenging conventional wisdom, latent class growth analyses found that **67.6%** of couples experienced insignificant satisfaction change over 20 years (Anderson, Van Ryzin, & Doherty, 2010), and over 80% of newlyweds experienced minimal changes over 4 years (Lavner & Bradbury, 2010). The narrative of inevitable decline applies to a minority of marriages. **Evidence strength: Strong.**

### How marriages fail at different life stages

**First two years.** Early dissolution is predicted by unrealistic expectations (Lavner, Karney, & Bradbury, 2013), negative communication patterns already present before marriage (Markman, Rhoades, & Stanley, 2010), high neuroticism, premarital parenthood, and what Stanley, Rhoades, and Markman (2006) call "sliding versus deciding" — couples who cohabit before a clear commitment and then marry through inertia rather than deliberate choice. In unstable marriages, satisfaction declined approximately **15% per year** versus **3% per year** in stable ones.

**Five to ten years.** The transition to parenthood is the dominant stressor. Twenge, Campbell, and Foster's (2003) meta-analysis in *Journal of Marriage and Family* found parents report lower marital satisfaction than nonparents (**d = −0.19**), with only **38% of mothers of infants** reporting high satisfaction versus 62% of childless women. Gottman's research found approximately **69% of marital problems are perpetual** — personality-based differences that never resolve. Couples who manage these perpetual problems through dialogue rather than attempting to solve them fare better. **Evidence strength: Moderate** (less research targets this timeframe specifically).

**Fifteen-plus years (gray divorce).** Brown and Lin (2012, *Journals of Gerontology*) documented that the gray divorce rate among adults 50+ roughly **doubled between 1990 and 2010**. Risk factors include higher-order marriages (60% of second marriages end in divorce), empty-nest and retirement transitions, health decline (particularly the wife's health; Karraker & Latham, 2015), and a cultural shift toward marriage as personal fulfillment — making people less tolerant of unsatisfying partnerships. Women's standard of living falls approximately **45%** post-gray divorce versus **21%** for men. **Evidence strength: Strong for demographic trends.**

### Pre-marital assessment programs and their outcomes

The **PREPARE/ENRICH inventory** (Fowers & Olson, 1986, *Journal of Marital and Family Therapy*) demonstrated **80–85% accuracy** in discriminating which couples would be happily married versus separated/divorced three years after the wedding, replicated by Larsen and Olson (1989) with 179 couples. The ENRICH married-couples version discriminated happy from unhappy couples with **85–95% accuracy** across 5,039 couples. Important caveat: these studies used extreme group comparisons, likely inflating accuracy. The authors themselves warned against using the instrument to predict outcomes for individual couples.

Carroll and Doherty's (2003) meta-analysis in *Family Relations* found the mean effect size for premarital prevention programs was **d = 0.80** — the average participant was better off than 79% of non-participants. Stanley (2001) found premarital counseling reduces divorce risk by approximately **30%**. However, long-term effectiveness beyond five years remains unproven (Bradbury & Lavner, 2012). A paradox emerges from Williamson et al. (2014): couples who receive premarital education are subsequently more likely to seek couple therapy, possibly because increased awareness lowers barriers to help-seeking.

**Catholic Pre-Cana** programs lack rigorous outcome studies; no peer-reviewed longitudinal data with control groups was identified. **Islamic pre-marital counseling** represents an even larger gap — programs exist widely but essentially no peer-reviewed outcome evaluations have been published. **Evidence strength: Weak to absent for faith-specific programs.**

### Cross-cultural predictors show universal themes with cultural variation

Grau et al. (2025) found that predictors of marital satisfaction — attachment, stress management, problem-solving — operate similarly across **65 countries**, though their relative impact differs by sociocultural context. Sorokowski et al. (2017, *Frontiers in Psychology*) found that marital satisfaction levels do not differ notably among Muslims, Christians, and atheists in large cross-cultural samples, though degree of religiosity (regardless of denomination) consistently predicts satisfaction (Mahoney et al., 2001).

On arranged versus love marriages, the evidence does **not** support a simple superiority of either type. Kowal et al. (2024, *Archives of Sexual Behavior*) studied five non-Western populations and found no significant overall differences in love scores. Xu and Whyte (1990) found love matches had higher quality at every duration in China, but Epstein et al. (2013, *Journal of Comparative Family Studies*) found love can grow in arranged marriages through practices that create vulnerability. Context, autonomy, family support, and socioeconomic factors are critical moderators. **Evidence strength: Moderate — mostly cross-sectional, growing literature.**

## Area 2: Which compatibility dimensions predict outcomes and how strongly

### The overarching finding: similarity matters for values, not personality

Before examining individual dimensions, one meta-finding demands attention. Montoya, Horton, and Kirchner (2008) conducted a meta-analysis of 460 effect sizes and found that **perceived similarity** has much stronger associations with attraction than **actual similarity**. Dyrenforth, Kashy, Donnellan, and Lucas (2010) showed that after controlling for main effects of personality, similarity effects on satisfaction largely disappear. The popular notion that "birds of a feather flock together" is partially true for initial selection but contributes relatively little to satisfaction once relationships are established. The critical exception: **values and attitudes** (religion, politics, life philosophy) are the domain where actual similarity consistently matters. Personality similarity contributes negligibly after controlling for individual trait levels.

### The fourteen dimensions ranked by predictive strength

**Tier 1: Must-match dimensions with strong effect sizes**

*Communication capacity.* This is the strongest predictor category (**r = .32–.36** for demand-withdraw pattern; Gottman claims 93.6% divorce prediction accuracy from Four Horsemen). However, this is not a "matching" variable — it is a **skill variable**. What matters is not matching communication styles but whether both partners commit to constructive conflict resolution. Communication patterns are also the most modifiable predictor through intervention. **Similarity vs. complementarity: Neither — quality matters, not match.**

*Financial behavior.* Financial disagreements predict divorce more strongly than disagreements about household tasks, sex, in-laws, or time spent together (Dew, Britt, & Huston, 2012, *Family Relations*, N = 4,500+ couples). Women who argued "often" about money were **nearly three times more likely to divorce** (Britt & Huston, 2012, 25-year longitudinal study). Money conflicts constituted only 19% of all conflicts but were rated more stressful, lasted longer, and were less likely to be resolved (Papp, Cummings, & Goeke-Morey, 2009). Rick, Small, and Finkel (2011) found that "tightwads" and "spendthrifts" tend to marry each other but these differences predict financial conflict and diminished well-being. **Must-match. Similarity strongly favored.**

*Core values and worldview.* Religious and political similarity is among the strongest similarity effects in the literature. Lehrer and Chiswick (1993, *Demography*) found religious compatibility's influence on marital stability rivals the magnitude of age at marriage — same-faith LDS couples had a **13% five-year divorce rate versus 40% for interfaith** couples. Luo and Klohnen (2005, *JPSP*, N = 291 newlywed couples) found couples show strongest assortative mating on attitudes, values, and religiosity, and value similarity predicted marital quality. **Must-match. Similarity strongly favored — this is the domain where actual similarity matters most.**

*Family planning.* Agreement on whether to have children is a **non-negotiable** where no compromise position exists. Twenge et al.'s (2003) meta-analysis found the parenthood effect on satisfaction at **d = −0.19**. The transition to parenthood produces a medium decrease in marital satisfaction. Number and timing are more negotiable than the fundamental decision. **Absolute must-match.**

*Religious practice level.* Mahoney, Pargament, and colleagues (1999, *Journal of Family Psychology*) found that over **42% of variance** in marital satisfaction related to sanctification of marriage — viewing it as sacred. Shared religious practices (praying together, shared worship attendance) predict satisfaction through spiritual intimacy. DeMaris, Mahoney, and Pargament found higher sanctification **buffered** adverse effects of perceived inequity. The degree of practice matters more than specific denomination. **Must-match on intensity level. Similarity strongly favored.**

**Tier 2: Important dimensions with moderate effect sizes**

*Intimacy and affection expectations.* McNulty, Wenner, and Fisher (2016, *Archives of Sexual Behavior*) found sexual satisfaction and marital satisfaction are **bidirectionally positively associated** across two longitudinal studies of 207 marriages. A meta-analysis of 93 studies (N = 38,499) found **r = .37–.49** between sexual communication and relationship/sexual satisfaction. Sexual desire discrepancy consistently links to lower satisfaction for both partners. Must-match on desire levels; specific preferences are negotiable through communication. **Similarity in desire level favored.**

*Health and substance use.* Homish and Leonard (2005, 2007) found in longitudinal newlywed studies (N = 634 couples) that discrepancies in heavy drinking and smoking predicted **significant reductions** in satisfaction over seven years. Concordant drinking couples report higher satisfaction. Neuroticism, the strongest negative personality predictor, is intertwined with mental health. **Must-match on substance use patterns. Similarity strongly favored.**

*Personal growth orientation.* Finkel's "Suffocation Model" (2014, *Psychological Inquiry*) argues that modern marriages must fulfill self-actualization needs, requiring both partners to invest substantial psychological energy. Partners who hold **growth beliefs** (relationships require work and can improve) show better outcomes than those with **destiny beliefs** (compatibility is fixed). Both partners need shared willingness to grow and adapt. **Must-match. Similarity required.** Evidence strength: Moderate-high; theoretical importance exceeds empirical precision.

*Household labor division.* The key mediator is **perceived fairness**, not actual division (Hochschild, 1989; Frisco & Williams, 2003). Even unequal divisions can be perceived as fair. Both complementary (breadwinner/homemaker) and egalitarian arrangements work if perceived as fair. Gender ideology moderates the relationship — egalitarian women are more distressed by unequal divisions (Greenstein, 1996). **Important but negotiable.**

*Work-life balance.* A BYU meta-analysis of 49 samples found work-family conflict and couple relationship quality have a negative relationship (**r = −.19**). Couples need aligned expectations about work commitment, but both complementary and dual-career models can work with mutual agreement. **Important but negotiable.**

**Tier 3: Nice-to-have dimensions with small effect sizes**

*Social life and personality.* Personality similarity (including extraversion match) has minimal effects after controlling for main effects (Dyrenforth et al., 2010). Individual trait levels (both being agreeable) matter more than similarity. Social network approval significantly affects relationship quality (Sprecher & Felmlee, 2000). On dominance-submissiveness specifically, complementarity may help (Markey & Markey, 2007). **Nice-to-have.**

*Life goals.* Shared life goals predict satisfaction, but this is often measured indirectly. Kurdek (1993) found couples who divorced by their fifth anniversary had larger differences on motives for being married. Must-match on fundamental direction; specific career paths are negotiable. **Evidence strength: Moderate.**

*Aesthetic and lifestyle preferences.* This represents an **acknowledged research gap**. Few peer-reviewed studies directly examine aesthetic preferences and marital outcomes. Clinically, these issues tend to be symptoms of deeper disagreements about respect and consideration. **Nice-to-have — among the most negotiable dimensions.**

### Deal-breakers operate through accumulation, not binary rejection

Jonason, Garcia, Webster, Li, and Fisher (2015, *Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin*, N > 6,500) established that negative information outweighs positive information in mate selection — consistent with Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory. Women report more deal-breakers than men. People with higher mate value report more deal-breakers. Joel and Charlot (2022) found that a single deal-breaker was **not** grounds for complete rejection — deal-breakers function more like "dealbenders" that accumulate.

Non-negotiable deal-breakers supported by evidence: whether to have children, fundamental values/religion mismatch, active substance abuse, violence, and basic financial philosophy. Negotiable with effort: communication skills (trainable), household labor division, aesthetic preferences, social life balance. Partially negotiable: sexual desire discrepancy, work-life balance, extended family involvement.

## Area 3: The science reveals a persistent gap between what people say and what actually matters

### Stated preferences fail to predict real-world attraction

The most consequential finding for platform design comes from Eastwick and Finkel (2008, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*): participants' stated ideal preferences, assessed before speed-dating events, **failed to predict what inspired their actual desire** when meeting real people. Men stated they valued attractiveness more and women stated they valued earning prospects more, but these sex differences vanished entirely in actual behavior. This has been independently replicated by Selterman et al. (N = 307) and confirmed by Todd et al. (2007, *PNAS*). **Evidence strength: Very strong.**

Eastwick, Eagly, Finkel, and Johnson (2011, *JPSP*) demonstrated a striking **double dissociation**: explicit (stated) preferences predicted attraction to photographs but not to live partners, while implicit preferences showed the opposite pattern — predicting attraction to live partners but not to photographs. Implicit and explicit preferences were completely uncorrelated (average **r = .00**). This means traditional questionnaires capture a preference system that operates in hypothetical evaluation but not in real-world romantic contexts.

Joel, Eastwick, and Finkel's (2017, *Psychological Science*) machine learning study attempted to predict romantic desire using 100+ self-reported traits and preferences. The algorithms could predict who likes others in general and who is generally liked, but **could not predict relationship-specific desire** — which specific individuals would be attracted to each other. Lead author Joel stated: "I didn't expect we would find zero."

### What actually predicts satisfaction differs from what people report wanting

Joel et al. (2020, *PNAS*) — the largest study of its kind across 43 datasets, 29 labs, and 11,196 couples — found the top predictors of relationship quality were **perceived partner commitment, appreciation, sexual satisfaction, perceived partner satisfaction, and low conflict**. Demographics like income, education, and age had relatively little predictive power. The critical insight: "Who I am" doesn't matter much once you know "who I am when I am with you."

Physical attractiveness shows contested effects. Meltzer et al. (2014, *JPSP*) found husbands were more satisfied with physically attractive wives, but Eastwick et al.'s (2014) meta-analysis of approximately 97 samples and 30,000 participants found these sex differences were "extremely small and nonsignificant." Income has weak-to-moderate direct effects on satisfaction; financial **strain** (subjective experience) is a more robust predictor than income level (Johnson et al., *PMC5464617*). OkCupid data confirmed the stated-revealed gap at scale: users' answers showed progressive racial attitudes, but actual messaging behavior showed persistent racial biases.

### Evidence-based frameworks for necessities versus luxuries

Li et al. (2002, *JPSP*) introduced the influential "mate dollars" budget-allocation paradigm. When budgets were constrained, people prioritized **necessities** first: physical attractiveness for men, status/resources for women, and kindness and intelligence for both sexes. When budgets were large, spending on necessities plateaued and people invested in "luxuries" (creativity, humor). This framework has been replicated cross-culturally (Li et al., 2011; Thomas et al., 2020).

Fletcher et al.'s (1999, *JPSP*) Ideal Standards Model organized partner ideals into three factors: warmth-trustworthiness, vitality-attractiveness, and status-resources. Discrepancies in the **warmth/trustworthiness** dimension were the most robust predictors of relationship dissatisfaction across replications (Campbell et al., 2001). Critically, Campbell et al. (2001) found that **individuals with more flexible standards were more satisfied** even when partners didn't perfectly meet ideals. A satisficing approach to partner selection is associated with greater relationship satisfaction and commitment, while maximizing is associated with persistent comparison and regret (Schwartz et al., 2002; Mikkelson & Pauley, 2013).

Buyukcan-Tetik et al. (2017, *Frontiers in Psychology*) examined threshold effects in a three-year longitudinal newlywed study: below-threshold discrepancies from ideals hurt significantly, but above-threshold gains were smaller — consistent with prospect theory's loss aversion. **People are more sensitive to partners falling below their minimum standards than to partners exceeding them.**

### Accommodation and flexibility predict outcomes better than initial match quality

Rusbult et al.'s (1991, *JPSP*) foundational six-study series established that **accommodation** — willingness to inhibit destructive impulses and react constructively when a partner behaves destructively — predicted relationship quality. Le and Agnew's (2003) meta-analysis (52 studies, N = 11,000) confirmed that satisfaction, alternatives, and investment all predicted commitment, and commitment was the defining feature of lasting relationships. Bui, Peplau, and Hill (1996) showed this model predicted relationship duration over a **15-year period**.

Felmlee's (1995, *Journal of Social and Personal Relationships*) research on "fatal attractions" found that in **29% of breakups**, the trait that initially attracted someone was the same trait that led to disaffection. The most common patterns: fun→foolish, strong→domineering, spontaneous→unpredictable. Fatal attractions were more likely the more dissimilar or extreme the partner's characteristics. Conversely, Murray and Holmes (1996, *JPSP*) found that positive illusions — seeing partners more favorably than partners see themselves — predicted greater satisfaction and had self-fulfilling effects over time.

## Area 4: Existing pre-marital assessment tools compared

### PREPARE/ENRICH sets the gold standard but remains proprietary

Developed by David Olson at the University of Minnesota, PREPARE/ENRICH is the most extensively researched pre-marital instrument, with data from **500,000+ couples**. It measures 10 core scales: communication, conflict resolution, partner style and habits, financial management, leisure activities, affection and sexuality, family and friends, relationship roles, spiritual beliefs, and idealistic distortion (a validity scale correcting for social desirability). The customized version adds SCOPE personality scales aligned with the Five Factor Model and a Couple and Family Map based on Olson's Circumplex Model.

The instrument uses **5-point Likert scale items** (approximately 125 items, 20–30 minutes). Scoring employs two key methods: Individual Revised (REV) Scores adjusted for idealistic distortion, and **Positive Couple Agreement (PCA) Scores** (0–100%) representing the number of items where both partners described their relationship positively. Cluster analysis of 5,030 premarital couples identified four types: Vitalized (28%), Harmonious (27%), Traditional (23%), and Conflicted (22%).

Strengths include extensive validation, multiple language versions, and customization for couple type. Weaknesses: entirely **proprietary** (methodology not publicly available), requires paid facilitator certification ($35/couple), and validation studies used extreme group comparisons that likely inflate accuracy. Alpha reliability ranges from .60 to .89; test-retest reliability .77–.92.

### RELATE offers the most comprehensive individual and contextual assessment

Developed at BYU by Busby, Holman, and Taniguchi (2001, *Family Relations*), RELATE uniquely assesses four contexts: individual (personality aligned with Big Five), family-of-origin, cultural, and couple. At **271 items**, it is the most comprehensive available instrument. Its distinctive feature is **self-and-partner personality cross-ratings** — both partners rate themselves and each other, enabling perception-gap analysis similar to T-JTA's criss-cross feature.

RELATE is self-administered online without requiring a facilitator (though therapist-assisted interpretation is recommended). A database of 20,000+ couples enables robust research. Cost is lower ($10–40/couple). Larson et al. (2007, *Journal of Marital and Family Therapy*) found significant positive effects on perceived relationship satisfaction, commitment, and readiness for marriage.

Weaknesses: very long (potentially burdensome), reports can feel evaluative, and the instrument was primarily validated on U.S. college-age populations at BYU, raising questions about generalizability.

### FOCCUS bridges faith and clinical assessment

Originally developed by the Archdiocese of Omaha (1985), FOCCUS uses a simpler **Agree/Disagree/Uncertain** format across 151 items and 19 categories organized into five areas: MATCH (lifestyle, personality), SKILLS (communication, problem-solving), BONDERS (religion, parenting, family, sexuality, finances), SUMMARY (key problems, family of origin), and SPECIAL GROUPS (interfaith, remarriage, cohabiting). Williams and Jurich (1995) found FOCCUS predicted relationship quality after five years with classification rates of **80% and 82%** — comparable to PREPARE/ENRICH.

Multiple faith-tailored editions exist (Catholic, Orthodox Christian, Non-Denominational, General). The simpler response format is accessible but less nuanced than Likert scales, and "Uncertain" responses are scored as disagreement, potentially penalizing couples who simply haven't discussed certain topics.

### The Taylor-Johnson Temperament Analysis uniquely reveals perception gaps

The T-JTA measures nine bipolar personality traits through 180 questions, but its distinctive contribution is the **criss-cross feature**: four administrations where each partner rates themselves and the other. Discrepancies between self-perception and partner-perception become powerful discussion catalysts. For instance, one partner may see themselves as "composed" while their partner rates them as "nervous." The instrument correlates highly with MMPI and 16-PF but is not specifically a pre-marital tool — it lacks relationship-specific dimensions like financial management or conflict resolution.

### Faith-specific tools represent a critical gap

**No psychometrically validated pre-marital assessment instrument specific to Islam** comparable to PREPARE/ENRICH exists. The only peer-reviewed validated tool for Muslim couples is the WQMCF (WIFAQ Questionnaire for Muslim Couples and Families; Soliman, Abdel-Salam, & Ahmed, 2022, *Humanities and Social Sciences Communications*), a 22-item, 5-factor instrument validated on 1,212 Muslim individuals in Qatar and Egypt — but it was designed for existing marriages, not pre-marital assessment. Informal guides exist (Imam Magid's 100 Questions, Suhbah Institute's 260 Questions) but lack psychometric validation.

No validated instruments specific to Jewish or Hindu pre-marital assessment were identified. Faith communities commonly adapt secular tools or rely on clergy-facilitated conversation. Dimensions unique to Islamic contexts include deen/religiosity alignment, Islamic role expectations, mahr considerations, halal lifestyle alignment, and Islamic education of children — none of which are captured by existing validated instruments.

## Area 5: People don't know what they need, and that's the core design challenge

### The self-insight deficit is well-documented and large

The most consequential finding for self-awareness platform design: **people demonstrate remarkably poor introspective access to their own romantic preferences.** Eastwick and Finkel (2008) showed stated ideals don't predict actual attraction. Joel, Eastwick, and Finkel (2017) confirmed with machine learning that even 100+ self-reported traits and preferences cannot predict which specific individuals someone will desire. Sparks et al. (2020, *Journal of Experimental Social Psychology*) found "negligible evidence that people desire partners who uniquely fit their ideals."

Eastwick et al. (2011) revealed the double dissociation: implicit and explicit preferences are **uncorrelated (r = .00)** and predict different outcomes. Explicit preferences predict reactions to hypothetical scenarios; implicit preferences predict reactions to real people. Wilson and Gilbert's (2003) affective forecasting framework explains why: the experiential system that governs actual romantic experience differs fundamentally from the rational system used for prediction. People overestimate the impact of specific partner traits (the "focusing illusion") and underestimate psychological adaptation.

### Designing assessments that bypass conscious self-report

Five evidence-based design approaches emerge from the literature:

- **Forced-choice trade-off formats** (conjoint analysis, MaxDiff) force realistic trade-offs between partner qualities, revealing preference hierarchies obscured by Likert-scale ratings where everything gets rated as "important." Li et al.'s (2002) "mate dollars" paradigm demonstrated this principle: when resources are constrained, people reveal their true priorities.

- **Scenario-based vignettes** present concrete relationship situations rather than abstract ratings, activating the experiential processing system more relevant to actual romantic decision-making. Consistency across similar scenarios can flag areas of low self-awareness.

- **Response latency analysis** leverages the IAT principle: faster responses indicate more automatic, genuine preferences. The Partner Go/No-Go Association Task (P-GNAT; Eastwick et al., 2011) successfully predicted live attraction where explicit measures failed.

- **Self-other perception comparisons** (used by RELATE and T-JTA) reveal gaps between how people see themselves and how partners see them — a proxy for self-awareness in relational contexts.

- **Social desirability correction** through instruments like PREPARE/ENRICH's Idealistic Distortion scale, forced-choice formats that prevent uniformly desirable responses, or cognitive-load techniques that reduce strategic editing (Stodel, 2015).

**Research gap**: While discrete choice experiments are well-established in health economics and marketing, their systematic application to romantic partner preference assessment remains underdeveloped but represents perhaps the most promising methodology for a compatibility platform.

### Validated instruments for relationship-relevant self-awareness

**Attachment style** is the most predictive individual-difference construct for relationship outcomes. The **ECR-R** (Experiences in Close Relationships–Revised; Fraley, Waller, & Brennan, 2000) measures attachment anxiety and avoidance through 36 items. Meta-analytic evidence (Candel & Turliuc, 2019, *Personality and Individual Differences*) confirms both dimensions negatively correlate with relationship satisfaction. The **AAI** (Adult Attachment Interview) captures unconscious attachment representations through discourse analysis rather than self-report content, but requires approximately 14 hours per administration (interview, transcription, coding) — impractical for assessment contexts. The ECR-R and AAI show surprisingly low convergence, measuring overlapping but distinct constructs.

**Emotional intelligence** shows a meta-analytic correlation of **r = .373** with romantic relationship satisfaction (Jardine, Vannier, & Voyer, 2022, *Personality and Individual Differences*, 90 effect sizes). Brackett, Warner, and Bosco (2005, *Personal Relationships*) found MSCEIT scores correlated with marital satisfaction at **r = .51**. Self-report EI measures show stronger associations than ability-based measures, possibly due to shared method variance.

**Relationship readiness** lacks a single widely validated individual assessment. RELATE includes a companion **READY** assessment, and Larson and Holman (1994) identified three predictor categories: background/contextual factors, individual traits, and couple interaction processes. The Stages of Relationship Change Questionnaire (SRCQ; Schneider, 2003; validated by Bradford, 2012, *JMFT*) measures readiness for change across marital domains but targets existing relationships.

### A sobering limitation on self-awareness for partner selection

Joel et al.'s (2017, 2020) findings suggest a fundamental ceiling: relationship-specific chemistry may be inherently unpredictable from individual traits, so even perfect self-knowledge might not dramatically improve partner selection. What self-awareness can improve is: (a) screening out clear incompatibilities (values, deal-breakers), (b) recognizing insecure attachment patterns that drive dysfunctional partner choices, (c) calibrating expectations to be realistic, and (d) increasing willingness to seek help early. The strongest evidence for self-awareness benefits operates **within** existing relationships rather than at the point of initial selection.

## Area 6: What matching science can and cannot do

### No dating platform has validated its matching algorithm

Finkel, Eastwick, Karney, Reis, and Sprecher (2012, *Psychological Science in the Public Interest*) conducted a comprehensive 64-page systematic review and concluded definitively: **"To date, there is no compelling evidence that any online dating matching algorithm actually works."** Dating sites have "uniformly failed" to adhere to scientific standards for validation. eHarmony's "29 dimensions" (later "32 dimensions") have never been published in peer-reviewed form despite decades of claims. The Skeptical Inquirer (2014) found no published validation research. Robert Epstein asked in Scientific American Mind (2007) why a company claiming 12 million members would not validate its "scientific" test.

OkCupid's system is the best-documented approach: users answer questions with three components (their answer, acceptable partner answers, importance weighting on a 0–250 exponential scale), with match percentages calculated via geometric mean. Their 2014 experiments revealed something profound: when they told "bad matches" (30%) they were "good matches" (90%), **"people acted as if they were."** The suggestion effect was roughly half as powerful as the actual algorithm. Sharabi (2021, *Communication Research*) confirmed this: beliefs about algorithm efficacy predicted first-date success more than algorithm accuracy itself. **Evidence strength: Moderate for the placebo finding.**

### The Euclidean distance model is the most empirically supported matching approach

Conroy-Beam and Buss (2016, 2017, *PLOS ONE* and *Evolution and Human Behavior*) compared seven candidate algorithms and found the **Euclidean distance model** was the most evolvable solution for mate selection. Across studies of actual couples (N = 214, 259, 294), real mates lay close in multidimensional preference space to their partners' preferences. A cross-cultural study of **14,487 people from 45 countries** (Conroy-Beam et al., 2019, *Scientific Reports*) confirmed the universal pattern of mate choice is most consistent with Euclidean preference integration.

The Euclidean model has two desirable properties: it inherently **penalizes large deviations non-compensatorily** (a massive deficit on one trait cannot be fully compensated by excelling on another, unlike simple additive scoring), and it captures **diminishing returns** (reducing a large gap matters more than reducing a small gap). **Evidence strength: Moderate-strong.**

### Deal-breakers should be weighted preferences, not binary filters

Joel et al. (2022, *Journal of Experimental Social Psychology*) found that many traits considered "dealbreakers" (smoking, height, religion) **do not function as true disqualifiers** in sequential dating. Instead, they integrate into overall mate value assessments. Participants typically encountered at least two personal dealbreakers before choosing to reject. Bruch and Newman (2018, *Science Advances*) found mate selection operates more like elimination (no photo = 20x less interest; smoking = 10x drop), but this describes filtering behavior, not categorical rejection.

**Practical implication**: A platform should model deal-breakers as heavily weighted preferences within a Euclidean framework rather than hard binary filters, with the possible exception of a small number of true disqualifiers (wanting/not wanting children, active addiction, violence).

### Asymmetric preferences require independent distance calculations

When Person A cares deeply about trait X but Person B doesn't, the system should compute **each person's Euclidean distance independently** from their own ideal to the other person's actual traits, weighted by their own importance ratings, then combine. OkCupid's geometric mean approach handles this naturally: a question that is "Mandatory" for Person A but "Irrelevant" for Person B affects Person A's satisfaction heavily while barely affecting Person B's, and the geometric mean integrates both perspectives while penalizing asymmetry more than an arithmetic mean would.

Research from Stanley and Rhoades (cited in Joel et al., 2020) found that **perceiving one's partner as less committed than oneself** — even slightly — was among the best predictors of marital unhappiness. This suggests platforms should assess not just preference alignment but **intensity symmetry** — whether both people care about the relationship equally.

### There is no validated compatibility threshold, but a framework emerges

No research establishes a minimum compatibility score above which success is likely. The more accurate characterization from the literature:

1. A **baseline of shared values** (children, religion, financial philosophy) matters for viability — below this threshold, relationships face elevated failure risk
2. Beyond that baseline, additional similarity in personality provides **diminishing and possibly negligible returns** (From et al., 2025, meta-analysis of 339 studies: "the evidence does not clearly support actual similarity as a strong or universal factor")
3. What matters most — communication quality, perceived commitment, sexual satisfaction, conflict management — **cannot be assessed before two people meet** (Joel et al., 2020)
4. The **belief** that one is well-matched may itself improve outcomes through self-fulfilling prophecy (Sharabi, 2021; OkCupid experiments)

---

## Conclusion: Design implications from converging evidence

Five design principles emerge from this evidence synthesis that existing platforms largely fail to implement.

First, **assess what's assessable and be honest about what isn't.** Values alignment, financial philosophy, family planning intentions, religious practice level, and substance use patterns are meaningfully assessable pre-meeting and predict outcomes with moderate-to-large effect sizes. Personality matching, communication chemistry, and dyadic "spark" are not. A scientifically honest platform would focus on the former while acknowledging the limits of the latter.

Second, **use forced-choice trade-offs rather than Likert scales** for preference assessment. The Eastwick-Finkel findings render traditional "rate how important each trait is" questions nearly useless for predicting actual behavior. Conjoint analysis, MaxDiff, and budget-allocation designs reveal true preference hierarchies that standard questionnaires miss entirely.

Third, **weight the Euclidean distance model** as the scoring backbone, with deal-breakers implemented as steep penalty functions rather than binary filters. Compute distances independently for each person's ideal-to-partner-actual, weighted by individually reported importance, then combine via geometric mean to properly penalize asymmetric satisfaction.

Fourth, **build self-awareness as a primary product function**, not an afterthought. Include attachment style assessment (ECR-R), emotional intelligence measurement, perception-gap analysis (self vs. partner ratings), and explicit feedback showing users where their stated preferences diverge from their revealed preferences. The finding that self-awareness interventions increase help-seeking suggests the platform itself can serve a therapeutic function.

Fifth, **leverage the placebo effect deliberately and ethically.** The converging evidence that algorithmic beliefs improve outcomes (Sharabi, 2021; OkCupid experiments) suggests that communicating the scientific basis of matching — even imperfect matching — may itself improve relationship outcomes through increased confidence and reduced second-guessing. This is not deception; it is the well-documented power of expectancy in human relationships, which Murray and Holmes (1996) showed operates through the mechanism of positive illusions becoming self-fulfilling prophecies.

The largest research gaps for platform development are: validated pre-marital instruments for Muslim, Jewish, and Hindu populations; longitudinal outcome data for faith-specific programs; discrete choice experiments applied to romantic preference assessment; and — most fundamentally — whether any pre-meeting algorithm can predict the emergent dyadic processes that Joel et al. (2020) identified as the true determinants of relationship quality. The honest answer, as of 2026, is that no one has demonstrated this is possible.
