## 1. The Problem — Why This Exists

Most people approach marriage the way they approach a job interview: they prepare answers for questions they expect, present their best self, and hope for the best. The research says this is exactly backwards.

We're bad at knowing what we want. We're bad at predicting who will make us happy. And the tools we've built to help — traditional matchmaking, modern dating apps, casual questionnaires — are largely designed around assumptions the science has already disproven.

The gap is stark. **Traditional matchmaking** optimizes for family approval, socioeconomic fit, and surface compatibility. **Modern dating apps** optimize for engagement metrics and mutual physical attraction. Neither optimizes for what actually predicts long-term marital satisfaction. And most people walk into the most consequential relationship decision of their lives with no structured self-knowledge at all.

> **The most striking finding from 43 longitudinal studies covering 11,196 couples:** Who you are individually explains only 5% of your partner's satisfaction. How you function *together* explains 45%. Yet every existing compatibility platform assesses only the former.
> — Joel et al., 2020, *PNAS*

> **Pre-marital assessment programs reduce divorce risk by approximately 30%** and show a mean effect size of d = 0.80 — meaning the average participant is better off than 79% of those who skip it.
> — Carroll & Doherty, 2003, *Family Relations* (meta-analysis)

> **Financial disagreements predict divorce more powerfully than disagreements about sex, household tasks, or in-laws.** Women who argued "often" about money were nearly three times more likely to divorce — yet almost no pre-marital process addresses financial behavior in depth.
> — Britt & Huston, 2012 (25-year longitudinal study)

The problem isn't that people don't care about getting marriage right. It's that they don't have a rigorous, honest, science-backed process for doing so. Mizan is that process.

## 2. What the Research Revealed — Key Findings

### The Top Predictors of Marriage Success

The research converges on findings that are often counter-intuitive. Here is what actually predicts whether a marriage thrives or fails, ranked by evidence strength.

**1. Contempt — the single most lethal relationship behavior.**
Gottman and Levenson's observational studies identified four communication patterns that predict divorce with claimed 93.6% accuracy: criticism, contempt, defensiveness, and stonewalling (the "Four Horsemen"). Of these, contempt — communicating disrespect or moral superiority — is the strongest individual predictor of early divorce. This is a behavioral pattern, not a personality type, which means it is both assessable and, with effort, changeable.

**2. Perceived partner commitment, appreciation, and low conflict** — not demographics, not income, not personality similarity.
Joel et al.'s (2020) machine learning study across 43 datasets identified these relational variables as the top predictors of relationship quality. The implication is uncomfortable: most of what determines marital quality cannot be assessed before two people meet.

**3. Financial behavior alignment — far more predictive than income level.**
It's not how much money you have; it's whether you share a philosophy about money. Couples where one is a tightwad and the other a spendthrift report significantly lower marital well-being even when controlling for income (Rick, Small & Finkel, 2011). Financial conflicts are rarer than other conflicts but last longer, are more heated, and are less likely to be resolved.

**4. Neuroticism — the personality trait with the strongest negative effect.**
Karney and Bradbury's (1995) review of 115 longitudinal studies found neuroticism explains approximately 10% of variance in marital satisfaction — the largest personality effect. High neuroticism predicts negative marital interaction, which cascades into declining satisfaction for both partners.

**5. Shared core values and religiosity level — where actual similarity matters.**
Unlike personality (where similarity contributes almost nothing beyond individual trait levels), values and attitudes are the domain where actual, not merely perceived, similarity predicts satisfaction. Lehrer and Chiswick (1993) found that same-faith couples had a 13% five-year divorce rate versus 40% for interfaith couples. Mahoney et al. (1999) found that over 42% of variance in marital satisfaction related to sanctification of marriage — viewing it as sacred and shared.

**6. Disillusionment, not conflict escalation, kills most long marriages.**
Huston et al.'s PAIR Project — 13 years, 168 couples from courtship onward — found that what distinguishes couples who eventually divorce is not increasing hostility but the *decline of love and affection*. Most marriages do not collapse into war; they fade into indifference. Critically, **67.6% of couples experience insignificant satisfaction change over 20 years** (Anderson et al., 2010) — the narrative of inevitable decline is statistically wrong.

**What doesn't predict success (but people think it does):**
- Physical attractiveness — research consensus finds sex differences in its impact are "extremely small and nonsignificant" once properly controlled (Eastwick et al., 2014, meta-analysis of ~97 samples)
- Income level — financial *strain* predicts distress; raw income level has weak-to-moderate effects
- Personality similarity — after controlling for individual trait levels, personality similarity contributes negligibly to satisfaction (Dyrenforth et al., 2010)
- Cohabitation before marriage — once commitment quality is controlled, pre-marital cohabitation shows no protective effect and often predicts worse outcomes through "sliding" rather than "deciding" (Stanley et al., 2006)

### The Real Compatibility Dimensions

Research supports three tiers of compatibility dimensions — not everything matters equally, and not everything works the same way.

#### Tier 1 — Must-Match Dimensions (Non-Negotiable)

**Children — yes or no.**
There is no compromise position. Whether to have children is the only binary incompatibility with no middle ground. Disagreement here is not a compatibility gap; it is a categorical mismatch.

**Core values and worldview (religion, life philosophy, ethics).**
Must-match. Similarity strongly favored. This is the domain where actual similarity — not just perceived similarity — most robustly predicts satisfaction. Value congruence is the strongest assortative mating signal (Luo & Klohnen, 2005) and predicts quality longitudinally.

**Financial behavior and philosophy.**
Must-match on overall approach. Spender-saver discrepancies predict conflict even at high income levels. Transparency, debt tolerance, and whether money is "ours" or "mine" are foundational, not peripheral.

**Religious practice level and expectations.**
Must-match on intensity, not necessarily on denomination. Shared worship, shared sanctification of marriage, and congruent expectations for raising children in faith are stronger predictors than doctrinal agreement.

#### Tier 2 — Important, but Workable With Explicit Agreement

**Sexual desire level.**
Must-match on frequency and intensity; specific preferences are negotiable. Sexual satisfaction and marital satisfaction are bidirectionally and meaningfully associated (r = .37–.49 meta-analytically). Desire discrepancy is one of the most common reported sources of marital tension.

**Substance use patterns.**
Discrepancies in drinking and smoking behaviors predict significant satisfaction decline over seven years in longitudinal studies (Homish & Leonard, 2007). This is not moralism — it is pattern matching. Concordant couples (both drink or both abstain) report higher satisfaction than discordant ones regardless of which pattern.

**Personal growth orientation and therapy openness.**
Both partners need to share the belief that relationships require work and can improve. Research on "destiny beliefs" (compatibility is fixed) vs. "growth beliefs" (relationships require effort) shows the latter strongly predicts better outcomes. A partner who categorically refuses self-reflection or help-seeking when needed is a structural risk.

**Division of household labor.**
Workable through explicit agreement. The key predictor is *perceived fairness*, not actual division. Even unequal arrangements produce satisfaction if both partners experience them as fair. Egalitarian and complementary models both work — what doesn't work is resentment about unacknowledged invisible labor.

**Work-life balance expectations.**
Meta-analysis confirms work-family conflict negatively predicts relationship quality (r = –.19). Compatible expectations about career commitment, travel, and income priority matter — not which model you choose.

#### Tier 3 — Nice-to-Have (Genuinely Negotiable)

**Personality similarity** (beyond individual trait levels). Almost no residual effect on satisfaction.

**Aesthetic and lifestyle preferences** (home environment, dress standards, décor). The least researched dimension. Clinically, conflicts here tend to be symptoms of deeper respect or control issues.

**Social life balance** (introversion/extroversion match). Individual agreeableness matters more than similarity; negotiable through clear communication.

### Deal-Breakers vs. Negotiables — What the Science Actually Says

The research on deal-breakers delivers a complicated verdict. Jonason et al. (2015, *Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin*, N > 6,500) established that negative information outweighs positive information in mate selection — consistent with loss aversion. Women report more deal-breakers than men. People with higher mate value report more deal-breakers.

But here's the counter-intuitive finding:

> **Deal-breakers don't usually function as true binary filters.** Joel et al. (2022) found that most people encountered at least two personal deal-breakers before choosing to reject a potential partner. What people call "deal-breakers" typically function as heavily weighted factors that accumulate — not as on/off switches.

The practical implication: the *stated* deal-breaker list people bring into partner selection often doesn't match their *revealed* behavior. The platform must surface this gap.

**What research supports as genuine non-negotiables:**
Wanting vs. not wanting children. Active, unacknowledged addiction. Fundamental values incompatibility (e.g., one partner is deeply religious, the other is hostile to religion). A track record of domestic violence.

**What is genuinely negotiable with explicit commitment:**
Communication style (trainable through skill-building). Household labor division. Aesthetic preferences. Extended family involvement level. Social life frequency.

**What people think is non-negotiable but research suggests is more flexible:**
Height, income, occupation, education level parity — all have weak-to-negligible effects on long-term satisfaction. Age gaps matter more at extremes; moderate gaps show little effect.

### The Self-Awareness Gap

This is the finding that most directly shapes the platform's design philosophy.

> **People's stated preferences do not predict their actual romantic behavior.**
> Eastwick & Finkel (2008, *JPSP*): participants' ideals assessed before speed-dating events failed entirely to predict who they were actually attracted to. The stated sex differences (men valuing attractiveness more, women valuing earning prospects more) **vanished completely in real-world behavior.**

The problem runs deeper. Eastwick et al. (2011) found that explicit preferences and implicit preferences are **completely uncorrelated (r = .00)** and predict different outcomes. Explicit preferences predict reactions to photos and hypothetical descriptions. Implicit preferences predict reactions to real, live people. Most questionnaires only access the explicit system.

Joel, Eastwick, and Finkel (2017, *Psychological Science*) attempted to use machine learning with 100+ self-reported traits to predict romantic desire. Result: the algorithms could predict who is generally liked, but **could not predict which specific individuals would attract each other**. The lead author's summary: *"I didn't expect we would find zero."*

What does this mean for the platform?

It means self-report questionnaires cannot reliably predict dyadic chemistry. What they *can* do:
- Screen genuine incompatibilities (values, deal-breakers, family planning)
- Reveal insecure attachment patterns that drive dysfunctional selection
- Force honest trade-off thinking through conjoint-style formats
- Create a gap analysis between stated and revealed preferences through behavioral scenarios
- Calibrate expectations to be realistic

The goal is not to predict who you'll fall for. It is to help you *recognize* whether the person you fall for is actually right for you — and give you the structured vocabulary to find out.

### What Existing Tools Get Right and Wrong

**PREPARE/ENRICH** — the most validated instrument in the field (data from 500,000+ couples). Achieves 80–85% accuracy discriminating happy from distressed couples at three years. Covers 10 core domains including communication, finances, spiritual beliefs, and conflict resolution. *What we take from it:* its category structure and the concept of Positive Couple Agreement scores. *What we improve on:* it is fully proprietary, requires a paid facilitator, uses Likert scales that flatten responses, and has no meaningful honesty-calibration mechanism.

**RELATE** — the most academically comprehensive instrument (271 items, BYU). Includes individual, family-of-origin, cultural, and couple contexts. Cross-rating feature (each partner rates themselves and the other) is its most powerful element. *What we improve on:* built primarily on U.S. LDS populations; impractical length; no Islamic or other faith-specific validity data.

**FOCCUS** — the best faith-integrated tool, with Agree/Disagree/Uncertain format across 151 items. Validated at 80–82% predictive accuracy at five years. Multiple faith editions. *What we improve on:* "Uncertain" answers are coded as disagreement, penalizing couples who simply haven't discussed topics rather than couples who have discussed and disagreed.

**eHarmony's 29/32 Dimensions** — never peer-reviewed, never validated in published research. No independent scientist has examined the algorithm. The company's internal claims of better outcomes remain unverified. *What we take from it:* the insight that matching on multiple weighted dimensions simultaneously is more predictive than any single dimension alone.

**Islamic pre-marital tools** — no psychometrically validated pre-marital assessment for Muslim couples exists in the published literature. The gap between Islamic marriage guidance (extensive, sophisticated) and validated measurement instruments is real and large. Mizan's Islamic module will be among the first structured, evidence-informed instruments to address this directly.

## 3. Our Approach — What We're Building

### Phase 1: Self-Discovery Quiz & Personal Marriage Readiness Profile

The first product is a comprehensive, research-backed self-assessment. Not a personality quiz. Not a compatibility game. A rigorous instrument designed to surface *what you actually need* rather than what you think you want.

**The quiz engine covers all evidence-backed compatibility dimensions**, explicitly organized into Must-Match, Important, and Nice-to-Have tiers. Every item has a rationale traceable to published research.

**Honesty calibration is built into the design.** The quiz uses three mechanisms to surface authentic preferences:

- *Forced-choice trade-offs* (conjoint-style): instead of rating everything as "very important," you must choose between two desirable traits when you can't have both. This is directly informed by Li et al.'s (2002) mate-dollar paradigm, which consistently reveals priorities that Likert scales conceal.
- *Scenario-based vignettes*: concrete situations rather than abstract ratings, activating the experiential preference system more relevant to real relationships.
- *Social desirability detection*: items designed to flag when someone is presenting their ideal self rather than their actual self — similar to PREPARE/ENRICH's Idealistic Distortion scale.

**The output is a personal "Marriage Readiness Profile"**, exportable as PDF, image, or markdown. The profile shows:

- **Your Values Map** — where your core commitments sit and how non-negotiable they are
- **Your Compromise Flexibility** — dimension-by-dimension analysis of where you have genuine flexibility and where you don't
- **Your Blind Spots** — areas where your stated preferences diverge from patterns in your behavioral responses
- **Your Priority Pattern** — how your actual allocation of importance compares to what research predicts leads to satisfaction
- **Your Attachment Orientation** — based on ECR-R validated attachment measure, with practical implications explained
- **Your Readiness Assessment** — a structured view of readiness dimensions, not a score that flatters or discourages

The profile is honest by design. It does not tell you what you want to hear. It reflects what the research — and your own responses — actually reveal.

### Phase 2: Matching Engine (Future)

When a sufficient database of profiles exists, the matching layer activates.

**The algorithm uses a Euclidean distance model** — the most empirically validated matching approach in the published literature (Conroy-Beam & Buss, 2016; validated cross-culturally across 14,487 people in 45 countries). Distance between your ideal profile and a potential partner's actual profile is computed across all weighted dimensions simultaneously. Large gaps on high-importance dimensions are penalized non-compensatorily — a deficit on your highest priority cannot be cancelled out by a surplus on your lowest.

**Deal-breakers are implemented as steep penalty functions**, not binary filters. This matches how they actually work in human decision-making (Joel et al., 2022) while still giving them appropriate weight.

**Asymmetric preferences are handled correctly.** When you care deeply about Dimension X and your potential match doesn't care much either way, the algorithm computes each person's satisfaction distance independently and combines them via geometric mean — ensuring that your high investment in a dimension doesn't get diluted by your partner's indifference.

**Compatibility scores are transparent.** Not just "87% compatible." The output shows dimension-by-dimension alignment, identifies where the biggest gaps are, and surfaces which gaps research suggests are bridgeable and which are structural risks.

### Phase 3: Community & Guidance (Future)

The third layer extends the platform beyond assessment into structured preparation.

- **Pre-marital counseling integration** — structured conversation guides based on quiz results, designed to facilitate the discussions that PREPARE/ENRICH and FOCCUS research shows lead to better outcomes
- **Faith-specific modules** — Islamic, Christian (Catholic and Protestant), Jewish, and secular frameworks, each calibrated to the dimensions most relevant to that community's specific compatibility context
- **Success pattern learning** — as coupled users report outcomes over time, the matching weights are updated to reflect observed rather than predicted compatibility patterns
- **Community guidance** — curated resources, verified counselors, and peer discussion organized by the same evidence-based dimensions as the assessment

## 4. What Makes Mizan Different

**Research-first, not assumption-first.** Every assessment item, every scoring weight, and every recommendation is grounded in peer-reviewed research. Where the evidence is weak, that is disclosed. Where the evidence is strong, that strength is reflected in the weighting.

**Honesty over flattery.** The output tells you what the research — and your own responses — reveal, not what you want to hear. Most compatibility platforms optimize for user satisfaction with the output. We optimize for accuracy about long-term outcomes.

**Compromise transparency.** The profile does not present compatibility as binary. It shows you where your flexibility is real, where it is self-deception, and where there simply is no middle ground. This is how adults should make decisions.

**Self-awareness as the foundation.** You cannot find the right person if you don't know yourself. The self-assessment is not a gateway to the matching engine; it is the primary product. A person who completes it and uses it honestly is better equipped to evaluate any relationship — with or without the platform's algorithm.

**Cross-cultural and faith-inclusive validity.** The research base spans 65+ countries and multiple religious contexts. The Islamic module addresses a genuine gap in the existing literature. Faith is not treated as a demographic variable but as a substantive dimension of compatibility in its own right.

**No dark patterns.** No engagement optimization. No algorithmic nudging toward premium features. No artificial scarcity of matches. The business model aligns with user outcomes, not user time-on-platform.
